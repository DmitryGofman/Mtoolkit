(`action=query&prop=imageinfo&iiprop=extmetadata`) before download. Files saved to
`app/public/visuals/` with the `ch10-` prefix. Photos were downloaded as the ≤1280px

## ch10-print-bed-detachment-fail.jpg

**What it shows:** A failed 3D print photographed from above on a wooden table: a
partially-formed white/translucent part with a clearly visible diamond-grid infill
pattern on its interior faces, surrounded by large tangles of loose, curled filament
strands that came off the nozzle after the part let go of the build plate mid-print.
The uploader's own (Polish) caption translates to "Failed 3D print, effect of the
object detaching from the platform during printing" — i.e., a genuine bed-adhesion /
warping-style failure (not a classic "curled-up corner" warp, but the same underlying
failure family: loss of bed adhesion mid-print). Bonus image beyond the core 8 topics,
included because it's a strong, honestly-described real failure photo that also happens
to show a printed object's infill pattern (relevant to topic 7) in the same frame.

- Original file: `File:Nieudany wydruk 3D 01.jpg` ("Nieudany wydruk 3D" = Polish for

## ch10-sla-printed-part.jpg

**What it shows:** A single SLA (stereolithography, resin) printed part photographed
on a plain white background: a smooth, glossy, cream-white Y-shaped/branching bracket
with a round through-hole and a thin brown support-line visible along one edge (a
remnant witness mark from the build platform or support structures). The very fine,
glossy, almost injection-molded-looking surface finish is characteristic of SLA resin
prints and contrasts clearly with the visible layer lines of the FDM images above —
useful for teaching the visual difference between the two processes.

## ch10-sls-process-diagram.png

**What it shows:** A labeled schematic diagram of the SLS (selective laser sintering)
process: CAD model → CAM/slicing (shown as two computer icons on the left) → the powder
bed process itself on the right, showing a scanning/deflecting laser mirror (with a
laser-hazard warning icon), a roller that spreads powder across the build platform, and
two pistons (powder-supply piston rising, build/fabrication piston lowering one layer
at a time) with a cross-section of the part being formed in the powder bed. **Caveat:
this is a diagram, not a photograph of an actual SLS machine or a real nylon
sintered part** — I could not find a Commons photo of an actual SLS-printed nylon
part or a clear photo of a modern SLS machine's build chamber (see "not found" note
below); the one candidate machine photo I found (`File:3dprinter.jpg`, a 1990s DTM
SLS rapid-prototyping machine) was low-resolution (299×228 px) and not very
illustrative, so this diagram was chosen instead as the clearer teaching image.

- Original file: `File:Selective laser sintering principle.png`
- Commons page: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Selective_laser_sintering_principle.png
- Author: LaurensvanLieshout
- License: CC BY-SA 3.0
- Suggested topic: "SLS powder-bed part / nylon sintered part" (topic 6) — process
  diagram substitute, see caveat
- Type: Diagram (original PNG, 164 KB, no re-encoding needed)

## ch10-print-orientation-support-tradeoff.png

**What it shows:** A two-panel slicer-software comparison diagram: the left panel
shows a small bracket-like part oriented flat/upright on the build plate needing only
minimal support material (a couple of small red support blocks); the right panel shows
a similar (or the same) part tilted at a steep angle, requiring an extensive forest of
tree-like support structures to hold overhanging features during the print. Directly
illustrates how build/print orientation choice drives support requirements — the
closest available Commons match to "print orientation diagram," since I could not find
a dedicated print-orientation-vs-anisotropy (mechanical strength direction) diagram
specifically (see "not found" note below).

- **Topic 6 (SLS powder-bed part / nylon sintered part), as an actual photograph of a
  real part or modern machine.** Searched "SLS nylon 3D printed part", "nylon powder
  printed part", "laser sintered nylon part", "powder bed fusion 3D printer machine",
  "EOS Formiga 3D printer", "3D printed part covered in powder" and browsed
  Category:Selective laser sintering and Category:Powder bed and inkjet head 3D

  I used the labeled process diagram instead (`ch10-sls-process-diagram.png`).
- **Topic 8, specifically an anisotropy/mechanical-strength-by-orientation diagram**
  (e.g. showing weaker inter-layer bond strength vs. stronger in-plane strength).
  Searched "3D printing anisotropy", "3D printing layer adhesion strength diagram",
  "FDM build orientation", "print orientation strength FDM diagram" — no hits. I used
  `ch10-print-orientation-support-tradeoff.png` (orientation vs. support-material
  requirement, not orientation vs. mechanical strength) as the closest available
  substitute for the "print orientation" half of this topic — see caveat above. If
  anisotropic-strength is pedagogically important, this specific angle may need a
  hand-drawn diagram rather than a sourced photo/diagram.
- **A genuine "classic warping" photo** (a corner of an ABS/PLA print curling up and

  no relevant hits. `ch10-print-bed-detachment-fail.jpg` and
  `ch10-failed-print-spaghetti.jpg` are both genuine failure photos in the same family
  (loss of bed adhesion → spaghetti) but neither shows the specific "curled-corner"
