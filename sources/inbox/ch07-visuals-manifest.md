## ch07-machine-shop.jpg

**What it shows:** A real working machine shop (NOIRLab headquarters, Tucson, Arizona)
photographed down the central aisle. In the foreground a wooden workbench with a bench
vise, a rack of clamping studs/hold-down hardware, and cardboard parts boxes; along the
right side a row of manual machine tools (an engine lathe with visible chuck and
controls, a Hardinge machine, milling machines) with hoist chains overhead. Exactly the
"job shop" atmosphere the chapter describes — benches, machines, work in progress, not
a showroom.

## ch07-cmm-inspection.jpg

**What it shows:** A Mitutoyo "Crysta" bridge-type coordinate measuring machine (CMM)
in a metrology room: large black granite table, moving bridge, vertical ram with an
articulating touch-probe head (stylus visible), a rotary table on the right, a jog
pendant (manual controller) lying on the granite, and the operator's computer
workstation in the background. A small dark fixture sits under the probe, but **no
workpiece is clearly seen mid-measurement** — caption it as "CMM in a metrology room"
rather than "CMM measuring a part." Converted from the original 2048px PNG to a
1280px JPEG to fit the size budget.

## ch07-welder-at-work.jpg

**What it shows:** A craftsman in Accra, Ghana, crouched low and welding/brazing the
bottom seam of a large sheet-metal panel (looks like a gate or tank wall) — torch in
hand, bright arc/flame and a shower of sparks, riveted/stitched seam visible along the
panel bottom. A genuine, unstaged small-workshop scene. **Safety note for the caption:**
he wears only dark glasses, no welding helmet or gloves — either crop/caption around it
or use it deliberately as a "real-world shops don't always look like the textbook"
discussion point. Do not present it as PPE best practice.

- Original file: `File:A welder at work welding a metal together (1).jpg`

## ch07-lathe-machinist.jpg

**What it shows:** Black-and-white Bundesarchiv photo (VEB Stahl- und Walzwerk
Brandenburg, East Germany, 1954): an older master machinist in a suit and a young
apprentice in work overalls leaning together over a lathe — quick-change/turret
toolpost with multiple tools in the foreground, a knurled workpiece held in the 3-jaw
chuck, chips on the ways. Reads naturally as "experienced craftsman explaining the job
to a junior" — good for the shop-floor communication / respect-the-craftsman theme.
Historic (1954) and monochrome; caption should say so.

exactly "people talking over an engineering drawing." Historic and monochrome; small

## ch07-incoming-inspection-tag.jpg

**What it shows:** A close-up of a real incoming-inspection (IQC) tag glued to a brown
carton at Lian Li Industrial (Taiwan, 2016). The red-and-white form has printed fields
(part number, part name, supplier, delivery date, quantity, IQC disposition) filled in
by hand — the part is an "HDD bracket," and the quantity has been crossed out and
corrected in pen, with an inspector's mark over the IQC field. **All text is in
Chinese** — treat it as an authentic artifact ("this is what a real goods-receipt tag
looks like") and explain the fields in the Hebrew caption; the students don't need to
read it. Genuinely great for the incoming-inspection / traceability discussion,
including the corrected quantity as a talking point.

- Original file: `File:Lian Li Industrial incoming inspection tag 20160809a.jpg`
- Commons page: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Lian_Li_Industrial_incoming_inspection_tag_20160809a.jpg
- Author: Solomon203 (Wikimedia Commons user)
- License: CC BY-SA 4.0
- Suggested topic: Incoming goods inspection (topic 4)
- Type: Photo (document/artifact close-up)

## ch07-first-article-inspection.jpg

**What it shows:** A QC inspector in a pink cleanroom smock and cap at a dedicated
first-article-inspection station (machine labeled "FAI-JCX830", Foxconn India): she
holds a populated circuit board (PCBA) in one hand while comparing it against the
reference layout/drawing shown on the FAI machine's screen; binders of inspection
records on the shelf above. **This is electronics manufacturing, not machining** — but
it is a real, labeled first-article-inspection process (part vs. reference document,
dedicated station, records), which is exactly the FAI concept the chapter teaches.
Caption it honestly as an electronics-factory FAI example. Converted from PNG to JPEG;
original is 857×720 (modest resolution, fine for an inline figure).
