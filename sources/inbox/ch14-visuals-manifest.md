## ch14-industrial-sewing-machine.jpg

**Shows**: A modern industrial flatbed sewing machine (branded "JUKKY," a
walking-foot / lockstitch machine typical of upholstery and heavy-fabric work)
on its table stand, photographed straight-on in a small workshop. Thread spools,

underneath. No fabric is actively being sewn in the frame, but the machine head,
tension dials, presser foot, and table-mounted clutch motor are all clearly

## ch14-lockstitch-diagram.png

**Shows**: A simple labeled diagram of a 301 lockstitch (the standard stitch
formed by nearly all industrial and domestic sewing machines): a gray
cross-section block labeled "Cloth" with a green "Upper thread" arcing over the
top and a red "Lower thread" (bobbin thread) arcing under the bottom, the two
threads interlocking inside the cloth at each stitch. Clean, minimal, textbook-
style vector-look diagram — no photographic ambiguity, single concept per
image.

**Commons page**: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Lockstitch_-_Anthony_Atkielski.png
**Author**: Agateller (Anthony Atkielski)
**License**: Public domain
**Suggested topic**: 2 — Seam types closeup / stitch types diagram
**Type**: Diagram/plate (PNG, 20 KB). **Caveat**: this shows *one* stitch type
(lockstitch, the mechanically important one for "how a sewing machine forms a
stitch") rather than a comparison of multiple seam/stitch types — see gaps
below for why a broader "seam types" diagram wasn't found.

## Topics I could NOT find a strong dedicated image for / gaps

- **Topic 2, a genuine "seam types" comparison diagram** (e.g., flat-felled
  seam vs. lap seam vs. bound seam, as used in webbing/soft-goods
  construction). Searched "seam types diagram", "flat felled seam diagram",
  "types of seams sewing", "stitch types diagram" — Commons turned up mostly
  19th/early-20th-century OCR'd book scans (sewing manuals, needlework
  textbooks) with no standalone extractable diagram, and no modern vector
  "seam types" comparison chart. Used `ch14-lockstitch-diagram.png` instead,
  which is a strong, clean diagram of *how a lockstitch is formed* — genuinely
  useful for the "how does an industrial sewing machine actually sew"
  question — but it is not a seam-*types* comparison. If a broader
  seam-types image is wanted later, a manual re-check of Commons categories
  `Category:Seams (sewing)` or German-language "Nahtarten" is the logical next
  search.
- **Topic 4, alternative textures**: `File:Blue_cordura_garment.jpg` (CC
  BY-SA 3.0, closeup of blue Cordura-style ballistic-nylon weave — passed the
  license check but not downloaded, since the ripstop image already covers
  topic 4 and shows the more distinctive ripstop grid) is available as a
  bonus/companion fabric-texture image if a second technical-fabric closeup is
  wanted.
- **Topic 6, alternative framing**: `File:Cursore_zip_con_sicura.jpg` (CC
  BY-SA 3.0, a zipper slider/pull on denim — more immediately recognizable as
  "a zipper" than the teeth-only macro, since it shows the slider mechanism)
  passed the license check and is available as a swap-in if a more
  obviously-a-zipper image is preferred over the pure teeth macro.
- **Topic 5, alternative**: `File:Climbing_harness_1981.jpeg` (CC BY-SA 3.0)
  and `File:Kong_-_ferrata_full.jpg` (CC BY 2.0) are both harnesses shown
  unworn (product-style shots against a backdrop) — passed the license check
  but not used, since the NIOSH construction-harness photo more literally
  satisfies "in use."
- A dedicated **"Nähmaschine Industrie" German-search hit**,
  `File:Fotothek_df_roe-neg_0006069_006_Arbeiter_an_einer_Industriennähmaschine.jpg`
  (CC BY-SA 3.0 DE, 1950s Deutsche Fotothek archive photo captioned "worker at
  an industrial sewing machine"), was checked but rejected: the photographed
  apparatus — two large thread-wound rollers on a trade-fair floor — looks
  more like a warping/finishing machine than a conventional sewing machine
  with a needle, and I could not visually confirm the caption, so I left it
  out rather than risk mislabeling it.
